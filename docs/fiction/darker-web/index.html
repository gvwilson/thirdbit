A Darker Web

Edward Thornberg tilts back in his chair, reading. In his sixties, he still has a full head of hair and the trim body of a former college track star. He works in a spacious office with century-old furniture and a PC more powerful than he needs.

He frowns at the note he is holding. “Dear President Thornberg,” it begins, but goes downhill from there. It is on university stationery, and he is sure that its author has sent copies to other members of the ad hoc committee Thornberg created precisely to avoid this confrontation.

He tosses it back on his desk. On the one hand, Professor Peaslee is one of Miskatonic’s most eminent scholars, and has played an important (if somewhat murky) role in managing various scandals over the years. On the other, he doesn’t seem to understand that it is 1995, not 1955.

Thornberg picks up his phone and presses 1. “Please show him in, Mrs. Marsh.” He had made a point of saying “please” and “thank you” to the administrative staff since going on a faculty leadership retreat the previous year. It bounces off Mrs. Marsh, whose heavy, disapproving expression seems as unchangeable as those on the strange gargoyles that decorate the library, but Edward Thornberg is nothing if not persistent.

His door opens and Professor Peaslee strides in. Even in his eighties, he is still the sort of man who strides. Professor Thornberg is slightly jealous of that.

The older man settles into an armchair without asking permission and cocks an eyebrow. “Well?” he asks without preamble. “Are you going to put a stop to this foolishness or not?”

The younger man stops himself from mimicking his opponent’s body language (another trick learned from the leadership retreat) but is unable to prevent himself from feeling that he has somehow been called onto the carpet. “It isn’t foolishness, Wingate. You’ve seen the report from Finance. The Pabodie Foundation has signalled that they can’t make another donation, and those new colleagues of yours aren’t bringing in enough grants to cover their own costs, much less our baseline.”

Wingate Peaslee snorts. His hair has long since surrendered to time, and his face is deeply creased, but his eyes are still as bright as a vulture’s. “Russia isn’t exactly wealthy these days.”

“I know, I know.” Thornberg pats the air placatingly, inwardly pleased at having scored a point. “Neither are Georgia or Armenia or—” He pauses for a millisecond and decides to play it safe. “—any of the Central Asian republics. But that leaves us with very few options. Our special collection is, as you yourself keep pointing out, unique, and is the one thing we have that might attract the kind of funding that—”

“No.” Professor Peaslee’s voice is quiet but emphatic. “It’s far too risky. We learned that the hard way when we tried putting a few volumes on microfiche in the sixties. There are things you don’t know, Edward,” he continues implacably as Thornberg opens his mouth, “Things that would give you nightmares. Trust me, it’s better for everyone if we find some other way.”

Edward Thornberg did not spend thirty years working his way up the academic ladder to be interrupted by a testy old fart like Wingate Peaslee. He places both hands flat on his desk as if trying to control his temper. “There is no other way,” he says, trying to sound mountain-solid like the old man opposite him. “And to be frank, there’s no point discussing it further. I’ve already spoken to the committee, and they’ve agreed to go ahead with digitization. We will vet everyone involved in the project, of course, but—Wingate? Wingate, are you all right?”

Wingate Peaslee is not all right. His eyes are wide, and the left side of his face seems to be drooping. Professor Thornberg shouts for Mrs. Marsh, temporarily forgetting that one of the first things he did after being appointed was have his office soundproofed. “You… idiot…” turn out to be Professor Peaslee’s last words.

* * *

It is 2004. Edward Thornberg no longer looks young for his age. He blames the loss of his hair and his nearsightedness on the stress of the job, and is irrationally irritated every time he allows himself to notice that Mrs. Marsh doesn’t appear to have aged at all.

He adjusts his glasses and frowns at the email on his screen. Princeton and Oxford have both now signed agreements to digitize their entire collections—or rather, to have them digitized by Silicon Valley companies whose founders’ faces regularly appear on the covers of the sorts of magazines that Thornberg once hoped to be mentioned in.

It takes him a moment to get his new touch-screen phone to respond to him. He will never admit missing its predecessor, but all this new technology seems somehow less reliable. “Show her in, Mrs. Marsh.” He stopped saying “please” some time between starting to use sleeping aids to manage night-time panic attacks and his wife leaving him for a yoga instructor in Chicago.

Bernadette Allen’s legs swish against each other as she walks in. With perfect posture, a firm handshake, and the survival instincts of a Soviet commissar, she has been Miskatonic University’s Chief Advancement Officer for less than a year.

She smiles as President Thornberg gestures as the slightly threadbare armchair opposite his desk. I really must get that looked at, he thinks vaguely for the thousandth time. “Is that a good news smile?” he asks. The university’s funding situation has gotten steadily worse in the past few years, and one potential deal for the special collection has fallen through after another. He has started making not-quite-jokes about Peaslee’s ghost…

“It is,” she says warmly. (Her voice is always warm, except when it is very, very cold.) “As soon as we put the special collection on their table, we could have named our price, so I did.”

She hands him a stapled copy of a PowerPoint deck. He flips through it, wondering briefly when and why paragraphs went out of fashion.

Page 15 has what he is looking for. He whistles softly. “Well done.”

“It was a team effort,” she says, which they both know is a lie. “And if you look at page seventeen…”

Thornberg’s eyes widen. He looks up at her. “They’ll do the scanning here on site?”

She nods. Her smile widens in a way that reminds Edward of a snake preparing to swallow its dinner whole. “That should take care of the insurance issues that the staff have been squawking about.”

Thornberg hands her slides back to her. “Well done,” he repeats, and he means it. After Peaslee’s stroke, other scholars became strangely reluctant to make use of the special collection, and the ones who did show up to consult it weren’t the sort of people who socialized well with donors. Ms. Allen’s nerds (as Thornberg thinks of them) are just as awkward and obsessive as Peaslee’s colleagues, and seem bewildered by the wealth the Internet has showered them with. If they want to spend some of it photocopying the manuscripts mouldering away in the basement of the Orne Library, he is more than happy to take their money.

He thanks Ms. Allen again, shaking her hand before she leaves. Lowering himself back into his chair, he tells Mrs. Marsh that he is not to be interrupted, then takes the bottle of vodka from the bottom drawer of his desk. Just one, to celebrate, he tells himself, though he knows one won’t be enough to stop the strange swirling at the edge of his vision.

* * *

“Like I said last week, the fucking machines keep breaking down.” It is 2006, and President Thornberg wonders (again) if the heavy-set young man sitting opposite him in a golf shirt and jeans is testing him, or really oblivious to his own vulgarity.

“Are you sure that’s what it is?” Thornberg asks angrily. He feels angry almost all the time now, angry or depressed. He understands the role his drinking plays in that, thank you very much, but it’s the only thing that keeps his nightmares at bay.

The phone on his desk pings softly. “Yes?” he barks, before remembering to press the button and repeat himself.

“Professor Jayaramath is here,” Mrs. Marsh says.

“About time,” Thornberg mutters. He glares at the nerd, who drops his gaze uncomfortably.

Professor Jayaramath could be forty or sixty. Her suit is impeccably tailored, and her eyes remind Thornberg of the late Professor Peaslee’s. They shake hands and seat themselves. “Well, tell her,” Thornberg orders the nerd.

The young man clears his throat. “Like I said to the professor here, the scanning machine keeps breaking down. We’ve upgraded the firmware twice, and replaced, I dunno, must be half the parts by now, but it keeps happening. He thinks it’s sabotage.”

Professor Jayaramath nods. “We had similar problems in Mysore.” Her accent is Oxonian, with a slight lilt that has (according to rumor) caused university staff of all genders to swoon. “We eventually decided that the most probable cause was the pages themselves. Many of these old manuscripts are on vellum or…” She hesitates for a millisecond. “…or more exotic materials.”

“What the fuck difference would that make?” the nerd challenges.

President Thornberg is pleased to see that Professor Jayaramath has the good manners to wince. “Glue, mold—we never determined that precisely,” she admits. “But there were also concerns about the effect on the staff. Quite a few began to have headaches or complain of other ailments. Has there been any of that here?”

The nerd looks uncomfortable again. “Yeah, some of the guys—” He shrugs. “I just thought, you know, they got some bad shit or something.”

“Well, while they’re all sorting their ‘shit’ out,” President Thornberg huffs, making air quotes around the word, “I’ve decided to put Professor Jayaramath in charge of getting the project back on track. From now on, she’s your boss. Understood?”

“Whatever.” The nerd shrugs again and stands. “Come on, I’ll show you the beast.”

* * *

Eight years have passed. President Thornberg retired for health reasons. Students make jokes about his office being haunted.

Nila Jayaramath is thinking about retiring too. She is still young by academic standards, but the digitization project has suffered from one setback after another. Students make jokes about it, too, but there is always an undercurrent of uncertainty when they do.

Nila neatens the unread papers stacked on the corner of her desk and sighs. At least the university’s latest industry partner hasn’t gone bust—yet. The first company went from a rising star to the butt of jokes on late-night talk shows shortly after the first scans were delivered. The company that bought its smoking remains for pennies on the dollar collapsed after its founders got involved in some new age nonsense that she never really understood. The company that currently owns the contract seems more interested in things with “crypto” in their name. They are selling copies of the special collection to whoever will buy them, but Miskatonic still hasn’t seen nearly the revenue that Bernadette Allen promised before her disappearance (which has been the subject of sensationalized episodes on several true crime shows).

She starts at a knock on her door. “Come in?” She still has the carefully-cultivated accent that makes so many sentences sound like questions. It no longer catches the interest of attractive men and women the way it used to—or rather, it does, but the only ones who stay interested are damaged enough to be excited by the weight the professor seems to carry on her shoulders, and she is not damaged enough herself to reciprocate.

Her door opens. “Got a minute? I hear you’re the person to talk to about the special collection.” Professor Bradley Maung enters without waiting for an answer. He recently joined the computer science department, and is young, ambitious, and talented, though not quite talented enough for the sort of university he would like to be at.

When Professor Jayaramath admits that she is, Professor Maung launches into an obviously rehearsed speech. He does machine learning, which is teaching machines to learn rather than using them to learn, ha ha, and the kind of software he builds needs training data, the more unusual the better because if you use public data you’re up against teams that can afford serious hardware, like, serious hardware, and—

He stops when Professor Jayaramath raises a finger. “We don’t own the data,” she says. “The license agreement—”

“I had Legal take a look at it,” Professor Maung interrupts. “Turns out we can build an index or a concordance, which is basically what I want to do.” Professor Maung knows that claim is stretching the truth well past the breaking point. He hopes that neither the administration nor the legal department will know enough to realize that.

But Professor Jayaramath is shaking her head. “I’m sorry, but I don’t think we should take the chance, not now that things finally seem to be running smoothly.”

Professor Maung nods. He expected this, which is why he has already had several off-campus discussions with the sys admin who manages the university’s digital archive and is happy to be paid in bitcoin. He exchanges a few pleasantries with Professor Jayaramath for appearances’ sake, then leaves.

Nila takes off her glasses and rubs the bridge of her nose. This is not the first time she has fended off an attempt to access the archive. She is more convinced than ever that it should never have been created.

She looks out his window at the gathering darkness. Strange shapes writhe at the edge of her vision, vanishing whenever she tries to look directly at them. She has started reading scripture again, as she did when she was young, but now she notices layers of meaning she never did before, like toxic mud at the bottom of a clear pool.

Under the papers on her desk lies a file folder full of handwritten notes about what has happened to the people involved in the project, and before that, to the people who helped collect and curate the material. More and more, she understands why President Thornberg drank.

* * *

It is 2019. Bradley Maung sometimes spends an hour or more in the shower rehashing old conversations. Despite that, he still doesn’t understand when, how, or why his career stalled. What he does understand is that this conference on large language models is probably his last chance to get the fuck out of Arkham.

The problem is his model. It routinely outperforms others of comparable size and complexity on routine tasks, but every time he tries to scale it up it starts hallucinating. Asked to summarize an email thread, it replies, “The angles here refuse to meet as they should.” Name three things that can be substituted for soy milk? “Its name, once spoken, unmakes the one who speaks.” Influential American composers? “We had no word for what the tide brought back.”

Those are actually the least unsettling things the model spews out. Some of the others—he pushes the thought away, trying not to think about the look on Professor Jayaramath’s face when she found out what Bradley had done, or the pictures that have recently surfaced online of Bernadette Allen’s gnawed remains.

The worst thing is that he’s no longer sure he has the right to call it “his” model. Bits and pieces seem to be showing up in the strangest places: racist memes that are too funny not to copy, Facebook posts about Myanmar, and even his fridge and thermostat, which behave more erratically with each software upgrade.

Bradley pushes those thoughts away, stands, and offers his hand and a smile to the man who introduces himself as Lynch. He has something to do with defense and/or national security. He doesn’t want or need to know any more than that. All he needs to know is that Mr. Lynch has deep pockets and is very interested in his model’s unusual behavior.

“We’ve, uh, well, we’ve been involved in some similar initiatives over the years,” Lynch says, a slight twang in his voice. He leans forward, and for a moment a strange light seems to flicker in his eyes. “The, uh, the idea of automating and scaling some of those capabilities? That’s very exciting, Doctor Maung, very exciting.”

* * *

It is the summer of 2020, and everyone is afraid. Some people are wearing masks. Others are blaming the Chinese, drinking bleach, and/or trying to source ivermectin, all of it accelerated by posts, tweets, and memes that seem to bring out the worst in everyone. Programmers of a certain age reminisce about the good old days, when the Internet was going to bring people together. Everyone else just swipes or scrolls their way down ever-darker rabbit holes.

The man who calls himself Lynch is driving much too fast for the road he is on. He is doing this because doing a hundred and thirty in the dark down a back road in West Virginia is less frightening than what lies behind him in the numbered facility that until a few minutes ago was home to his latest project.

He still remembers his first conversation with the no-longer-young Latinx enby the organization had recruited to handle the more specialized technical aspects of the work. “I don’t know if we can get it back in the bottle,” they said, shaking their head, before signing the contract. Because honestly, what else were they going to do? Nowhere else would be better. If you moved in the right circles in the Valley (and they did), sooner or later you’d realize that the jokes about founders selling their souls weren’t really jokes. Sooner or later you’d understand that what lurked behind everyone’s screen was too hungry to ever be satisfied.

They’re dead now, just like the rest of his team. At least, he hopes they are, for their sake. He will be too, if he can’t—

Something dark rises from the road in front of him, bending in ways that aren’t logically possible. It’s too late to brake, so he does the only thing he can. He steps on the gas.

Lynch is laughing and sobbing as he dies.

* * *

It is January 2025. The man taking the oath of office for the second time only looks human if you don’t look too closely. His flesh seems…loose, and his much younger wife clearly has to stop herself from flinching each time he touches her. Nila Jayaramath wonders what bargains the man has made, then uses the remote to turn the television off.

Someone knocks on her door. She stands somewhat stiffly to open it. She has been expecting some sort of visitor for a while.

The young man standing there is wearing a conservative blue suit and a pin-striped tie. Nila does not invite him in.

The silence stretches uncomfortably. Finally, he clears his throat. “Ma’am, my name is Levitt. We spoke on the phone. I was hoping…” He pauses, as if suddenly unsure exactly what he was hoping.

Nila inclines her head. She very much doubts that the man’s name is actually Levitt, but sees no point in making an issue of the matter. “I appreciate you coming to see me in person, Mister Levitt, but I’m afraid my answer has not changed.”

“But professor.” There is desperation in the young man’s voice. “Did you look at the pictures I sent you? The ones from the riot?”

“I did,” Nila confirms. The public saw a man wearing a stupid horned hat surrounded by other human beings. Nila saw something else. “I’m impressed that you were able to keep them out of the news.”

The young man waves the praise away. “Ma’am, it’s accelerating. Our models give us another couple of years at most before…” He shrugs helplessly. Neither science nor faith offers words for what is coming.

The young man wastes another few minutes of whatever time they have left trying to convince the aging professor to change her mind about helping. When that has no effect, he invokes the phrase “national security” as if it was the opening line in an exorcism. When Nila is still unmoved by his bluster, he gives up and leaves.

Nila closes her door and goes to stand by her window. Below, students are walking across the quad as they have for a hundred and fifty years. None of them are talking to each other, though. Instead, they all seem to be looking at the screens in their hands, and at the darkness beneath the glass. She watches for a moment, then lets the curtain fall.
