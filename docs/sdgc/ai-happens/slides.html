When the Algorithm Comes for You

Jay's Question Is the Right Question

  • Learner persona for this course: worried AI will take their job
  • Legitimate fear, not paranoia
  • Rest of course gives tools to think about it clearly
    • Same fear in every previous wave: always partly right, always more complicated [Acemoglu2023]

What AI Actually Is

  • Large language models trained on text and code produced by people paid little or nothing
  • Produce plausible outputs
  • Understand nothing
  • Latest in a series of productivity tools that redistribute work without eliminating it
    • GPT-4 trained on scraped text without consent or compensation [TorresGebru2024]

Every Automation Wave Looked Like the Last One

  • Mechanization eliminated most farm labor
  • Factory automation eliminated most craft manufacturing
  • Office computing eliminated most routine clerical work
  • New jobs appeared each time
  • Not the same jobs, not everyone made the transition
    • US manufacturing: 19.4M jobs in 1979, 12.8M by 2023 [Acemoglu2023]
    • Hollowed out entire regions
    • No equivalent local job creation

Who Bears the Cost of Transitions

  • Gains from automation go to those who own the tools
  • Costs of transition go to displaced workers: retraining, years of lower wages
  • Distribution is a policy choice, not a law of nature [Acemoglu2023]
    • Acemoglu estimates roughly half of US income inequality increase since 1980 attributable to automation replacing mid-wage workers

The Intellectual Property Question

  • AI systems trained on writing, code, art, and music without permission
  • Who owns the training data: legally unresolved
  • As described earlier, IP law has always been an arena where power determines the outcome
    • NYT lawsuit against OpenAI (Dec 2023): first major test of whether training on copyrighted material constitutes infringement

Enshittification Applies Here Too

  • AI services currently cheap or free: companies burning investor capital to capture market share
  • As described earlier, the cycle runs: attract users, then extract from them
  • Cheap, useful AI will not last
    • OpenAI lost ~$5B in 2024 providing cheap API access
    • Extraction begins when competitors are starved of users

Regulatory Capture Is Already Underway

  • Major AI companies placed former employees and allies in key regulatory positions
  • Industry associations writing their own proposed regulations
  • As described earlier, the better-organized side consistently wins
    • Anthropic, Google, and OpenAI all submitted proposals to EU AI Act consultation exempting their most powerful models from strictest requirements

The Manufacturing Doubt Playbook Again

  • AI labs commission their own safety research and publish favorable results
  • Critics framed as alarmists
  • Harms described as edge cases
  • As described earlier, same strategy used for tobacco, lead, and ozone
    • OpenAI's safety team resigned in 2024
    • Commercial considerations overrode safety

Framing Displacement as Opportunity

  • "AI won't replace you; someone using AI will" shifts burden of adaptation to workers
  • Treats costs of corporate automation as an individual problem requiring an individual solution
  • The passion principle applied to survival
    • Same framing used for every previous automation wave: workers told to "reskill," not that the economy owed them compensation [Acemoglu2023]

What Collective Responses Have Achieved

  • Writers Guild of America won explicit AI provisions after five-month strike in 2023
  • SAG-AFTRA agreement included restrictions on digital replication of performers
  • Unions negotiated protections individual workers could not [Kelly2022]
    • WGA contract: AI cannot write or rewrite scripts
    • Scripts cannot train AI [Kelly2022]

Regulation Has Worked Before

  • EU AI Act requires transparency, human oversight for high-risk systems, bans certain applications
  • Canada, Brazil, South Korea, and UK all have AI governance frameworks in development
  • As described earlier, industry predictions about regulatory infeasibility have been wrong again and again
    • EU GDPR described as "unworkable" before 2016
    • Has produced ~$4B in fines since taking effect in 2018

What Jay Can Actually Do

  • Organize: collective bargaining, professional associations, open-source communities
  • Demand transparency: right to know when AI makes decisions about you
  • Support regulation requiring human oversight of consequential automated decisions
  • Question is not whether to fight but where and how
    • Same structure as every previous power contest in this course
    • Specific tools are AI governance frameworks, union contracts, and liability law
