Institutional Power

Essential Services Create Leverage

  • Institutions that provide essential services gain power over those who depend on them
  • Medieval church: control of sacraments (baptism, burial, marriage) made excommunication devastating
  • Leverage extends far beyond the original service
    • Pope Gregory VII's excommunication of Holy Roman Emperor Henry IV in 1076 forced the emperor to stand barefoot in the snow at Canossa for three days to beg forgiveness [Southern1990]

Platform Excommunication

  • Removal from a platform removes access to customers, tools, and markets
  • No due process
  • Decision is final and immediate
  • For businesses dependent on a single platform, removal is existential
    • Parler removed from Apple App Store, Google Play, and Amazon Web Services within 24 hours in January 2021
    • Lost app distribution, hosting, and payment processing simultaneously

What Platforms Claim Jurisdiction Over

  • Platforms claim that moderation, terms, and algorithmic choices are internal matters
  • Claim this with more confidence than any democratic institution
  • Parallels the medieval church's claim to jurisdiction over its own members
    • Meta's Oversight Board, created to review content decisions, has no enforcement power and can be overruled by the company
    • Advisory theater [Doctorow2022]

Competing in the Market You Control

  • Amazon both controls access to its marketplace and sells its own products in that marketplace
  • Inherent conflict of interest: platform can privilege its own products at others' expense
  • Department stores faced the same conflict when stocking their own brands over suppliers
    • Amazon's "Project Nessie" algorithm, revealed in FTC filings in 2023, designed to test how high Amazon could raise prices before consumers went elsewhere [Stoller2019]

Information Asymmetry Enables Copying

  • Amazon has access to sales data for all products sold on its platform
  • Has used this data to identify successful products and launch competing private-label versions
  • Sellers cannot see Amazon's margins
  • Amazon can see theirs
    • 2020 Wall Street Journal investigation documented specific cases where Amazon used third-party seller data to develop competing private-label products [Khan2017]

A Conflict Securities Law Already Prohibits

  • Securities brokers prohibited from recommending securities in which they have an undisclosed interest
  • Same conflict (recommending your own product from a position of information advantage) is permitted on platforms
  • Prohibition in one domain has not been extended to the other
    • Glass-Steagall Act once separated commercial and investment banking for exactly this reason
    • Its repeal in 1999 created the conflicts that contributed to the 2008 crisis [JohnsonKwak2010]

Consequential Decisions Without Your Participation

  • Credit bureaus make decisions about loan eligibility, rental applications, and employment using your data
  • You do not participate in this process, cannot consent to it, and have limited ability to contest results
  • Companies profit whether the data is accurate or not
    • FTC estimated in 2012 that one in five Americans had an error in at least one credit report significant enough to affect their credit score [Oneil2016, Pasquale2015]

Accuracy Is Not the Same as Fairness

  • Accurate data encoding past discrimination perpetuates that discrimination
  • A credit score based on historical redlining data accurately reflects the history but reproduces its effects
  • Distinction between accuracy and fairness matters for policy even when it does not matter for profit
    • FICO scores penalize "thin files" (insufficient credit history) [Pasquale2015]
    • Systematically disadvantages recent immigrants, young adults, historically excluded communities

Data Breach Liability Is Not a Deterrent

  • Equifax's 2017 breach exposed personal data of 147 million Americans
  • Settlement was $575 million
  • Less than two weeks of Equifax's revenue
  • When liability is structured as a cost of doing business, it does not deter careless handling
    • Equifax's stock price recovered within six months of the breach
    • Investors concluded that legal and reputational consequences were manageable [Pasquale2015]

Private Giving as Public Power

  • Large charitable foundations exercise public power over education, health, and policy
  • Do so without electoral mandate or removal mechanism
  • Power is real regardless of the quality of the donor's intentions
    • Giridharadas documented how the Aspen Institute's "win-win" ideology brings together philanthropists and politicians to produce conversations that generate goodwill without challenging power [Giridharadas2018, Reich2018]

Tax Treatment of Charitable Foundations

  • Donors receive an immediate tax deduction for contributions to foundations
  • Foundation retains direction over spending in perpetuity
  • Converts taxable wealth into permanently controlled institutional power
    • At the legal minimum payout rate of 5% annually, a billion-dollar foundation can operate essentially forever while the donor retains strategic direction across generations [Giridharadas2018]

Power Without Democratic Accountability

  • Gates Foundation has shaped global health policy in ways that national governments have not
  • Walton Family Foundation has shaped US education policy toward charter schools
  • Quality of the intentions does not substitute for democratic legitimacy
    • Gates Foundation spent $1B promoting small high schools in the early 2000s
    • When subsequent research found no academic benefit, it moved on and left school districts to live with the reorganizations [Giridharadas2018]

Direct Democracy in Practice

  • Porto Alegre, Brazil gave residents direct control over a share of the municipal budget from 1989 to 2004
  • Infrastructure investment shifted toward poor neighborhoods whose needs had been systematically deprioritized
  • Model spread to over 1,500 cities across five continents [Baiocchi2005]

Bottom-Up Legal Reform

  • India's Right to Information Act (2005) won through a campaign by the Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan, a rural workers' organization in Rajasthan
  • Gave any citizen the right to demand documents from any government body
  • Campaign bypassed elite lobbying entirely, organizing through village assemblies and public hearings [JenkinsGoetz1999]
