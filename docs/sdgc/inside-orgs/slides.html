Power Inside Organizations

Organizations Are Political Systems

  • Resources flow to those with power, not to those who contribute most
  • Organizations are not meritocracies
  • They are political systems with economic outputs
  • Understanding this is more useful than being surprised by it
    • Jeffrey Pfeffer documented this across decades of organizational research: power predicts promotion better than performance [Pfeffer1992]

The Winning Coalition

  • Leaders maintain power by sustaining a coalition large enough to govern but small enough to reward [BuenodeMesquita2011, Runciman2014]
  • Coalition's interests determine policy, not the institution's stated mission
  • Perks signal belonging to those inside the coalition while masking the arrangement from those outside
  • We will see later how the same logic applies to democratic governments when gerrymandering and voter suppression shrink the effective winning coalition

Mission Language Serves Power

  • "We're all family here" makes employment feel non-transactional
  • Family rhetoric signals belonging while masking the nature of the relationship
  • Interests served by the metaphor are the employer's, not the worker's
    • Tech companies replaced "employees" with "Googlers" and "Amazonians"
    • The name change is the tell [Crick2000, Goffman1959]

Shareholder Primacy Is a Legal Structure

  • Publicly-traded corporation legally structured to prioritize shareholder returns
  • Directors who sacrifice returns for other goals face legal exposure
  • This is a legal design choice, not a law of nature
    • Milton Friedman's 1970 essay arguing that corporate social responsibility is a "fundamental misconception" provided ideological cover for making this choice seem inevitable [Bakan2005]

Corporate Traits and Personality Disorders

  • Traits associated with corporate success overlap significantly with clinical psychopathy
  • Charm, risk tolerance, willingness to externalize harm, absence of remorse
  • A structural observation
  • Not a claim about the individuals who work in these organizations
    • Robert Hare and Paul Babiak found psychopathy rates in a sample of senior corporate managers were four times the general population baseline [BabiakHare2019, BoardFritzon2005, Dutton2013]

Why Replacing Leaders Changes Nothing

  • Bad structures reproduce bad behavior regardless of who runs them
  • Replacing individuals while leaving incentive structures unchanged is theater
  • Structure selects for the traits it rewards
    • Enron replaced its CEO twice before collapse
    • Fraud continued because the incentive structure demanded it

The Agentic State

  • In hierarchical situations, people shift into an agentic state: executing authority rather than their own moral standards
  • Milgram: 65% of participants administered maximum voltage because a person in a lab coat told them to
  • Responsibility located in the authority, not in the person acting
    • Adolf Eichmann's defense at his 1961 trial was precisely this: he was following orders, not making moral judgments [Arendt2006, Milgram1974]

Milgram's Finding Does Not Generalize Uniformly

  • Milgram's original experiments used a specific, culturally embedded form of institutional authority: a white-coated scientist in a US university laboratory
  • Cross-cultural replications find compliance rates ranging from under 30% to over 85%, depending on cultural context, the framing of authority, and the relationship between the parties
  • Tendency to shift moral responsibility in hierarchical settings is real
  • Degree to which it happens is not fixed
  • Varies with institutional design and culture, which means those factors can be changed

Eight Ways to Disengage Morally

  • Moral justification, euphemistic labeling, advantageous comparison
  • Displacement of responsibility, diffusion of responsibility
  • Dehumanization of those harmed
  • None of these require cruelty
  • They require only cognitive reframing
    • Albert Bandura documented all eight mechanisms in corporate, military, and institutional settings [Bandura1999, Ewing2017, Palazzo2025]

What One Dissenter Does

  • A single visible dissenter breaks compliance far more effectively than visible compliance reinforces it
  • In Milgram's variation with a dissenting confederate, compliance dropped from 65% to 10%
  • Dissent is most powerful when it comes early
    • Frances Haugen's disclosure of Facebook's internal research documents in 2021 followed exactly this pattern: one dissenter changed the political calculus [Frenkel2021]
