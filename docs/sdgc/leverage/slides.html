Lock-In, Leverage, and Legalization

Personal Information as Leverage

  • Scientology's auditing process gathers detailed personal information that members cannot later retrieve
  • This information can be used against members who attempt to leave or criticize the organization
  • Leverage is structural to the relationship, not incidental
    • Scientology's "Fair Game" policy, formalized in the 1960s, explicitly authorized using personal information from auditing sessions to "destroy" members who left [Lewy2011, Wright2013]

Systematic Responses to Criticism

  • Scientology's response to critics has included litigation, private investigation, and organized harassment
  • These responses are available to any institution with sufficient resources and willingness
  • Tech companies have retained similar capabilities
  • Targets are typically journalists and researchers
    • Uber hired a firm to investigate journalist Sarah Lacy in 2014 after she wrote critically about the company
    • Plan was exposed when an Uber executive described it at a dinner [Srnicek2016]

Reputation Lock-In on Platforms

  • Any platform where users build audience, reputation, or relationships that cannot be transferred creates equivalent dependency
  • A YouTuber's subscriber base, an Etsy seller's reviews, a driver's Uber rating all belong to the platform
  • Structural lock-in is not unique to religious organizations
    • An Etsy seller with five years of five-star reviews who moves to a competing platform starts with a blank record
    • Years of reputation-building stay with Etsy [Doctorow2022]

Ransomware as a Professional Service

  • Ransomware groups operate with specialization, affiliate models, market rates, and reputational incentives
  • Colonial Pipeline attack (2021) and WannaCry (2017) run by organizations with customer service functions
  • Extortion, if professional enough, is structurally indistinguishable from a fee-for-service arrangement
    • DarkSide group that attacked Colonial Pipeline provided victims with a "decryption support service" and refunded payment when the decryption software failed to work correctly [Schneier2023]

The Paying vs. Not-Paying Problem

  • Official policy discourages paying ransoms
  • Actual practice often requires it
  • Governments acknowledge that paying is frequently the only realistic option while publicly discouraging payment
  • Gap between official policy and actual behavior is not hypocrisy
  • It is an acknowledgment that the problem has no clean solution
    • FBI advises against paying ransoms while simultaneously maintaining a list of vetted ransomware negotiators that it refers victims to [Dudley2022, Schneier2023]

Cyber Insurance and Moral Hazard

  • Cyber insurance policies often pay ransoms, reducing the cost of attack for victims
  • Creates moral hazard: insured organizations invest less in prevention
  • Same dynamic occurred with kidnap-and-ransom insurance in the 1970s and 1980s
    • Lloyd's of London began offering kidnap-and-ransom insurance in the 1930s
    • By the 1970s, professional kidnapping networks had adapted to treat insurance payouts as predictable revenue [Shortland2019]

Which Pleasures Are Legal and Why

  • Alcohol and tobacco are legal in most jurisdictions
  • Cannabis and coca leaf are not in most
  • Difference does not track harm profile
  • Tracks the political economy at the time laws were written
  • Racial hierarchy and commercial interest determined legality, not pharmacology
    • Harry Anslinger's 1930s campaign against cannabis explicitly used racial language, associating it with Black jazz musicians and Mexican immigrants, and succeeded in producing federal prohibition [Hari2015, Keefe2021, Nutt2012]

Who Bears the Costs of Criminalization

  • Costs of drug criminalization fall on the least powerful communities regardless of use rates
  • Cannabis arrest rates in the US are several times higher for Black Americans despite similar use rates
  • Burden of enforcement distributed by race, not by harm
    • ACLU found in 2020 that Black Americans were 3.73 times more likely to be arrested for cannabis possession than white Americans
    • Disparity persisted even in states where cannabis was nominally decriminalized [Anderson2018]

Harm Reduction Consistently Outperforms Criminalization

  • Portugal decriminalized personal drug possession in 2001
  • Drug deaths and HIV infections fell sharply
  • Switzerland's heroin-assisted treatment program reduced crime and improved health outcomes
  • Evidence for harm reduction is consistent across multiple countries and policy contexts
    • Portugal's drug death rate fell from among Europe's highest to among its lowest within a decade of decriminalization
    • Policy has been in place for 25 years [Nutt2012]

The Same Substance, Two Responses

  • OxyContin addiction in white rural communities met treatment programs and empathy
  • Crack cocaine in Black urban communities in the 1980s met mandatory minimum sentences
  • Same addiction mechanism
  • Different political response
    • Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 set mandatory minimums 100 times harsher for crack than for powder cocaine
    • Fairness in Sentencing Act of 2010 reduced this to an 18-to-1 disparity [Kendi2016]

Every New Medium Triggers a Panic Cycle

  • Novels, penny dreadfuls, film, comic books, television, video games, and social media
  • Each identified as uniquely dangerous to youth by a coalition of moral entrepreneurs, politicians, and researchers
  • Pattern is the panic cycle, not the medium
    • 1954 Senate hearings on comic books produced the Comics Code
    • Industry self-censorship regime that lasted until 2011 and was never shown to have any relationship to youth behavior [Markey2017]

Obscenity Standards Never Stabilize

  • Justice Potter Stewart (1964): "I know it when I see it"
  • What counts as obscene has shifted with political context in every jurisdiction
  • Standards track political power, not evidence of harm
    • James Joyce's Ulysses banned as obscene in the US from 1922 until 1933
    • The judge who permitted its publication noted that the law's definition of obscenity had no relationship to actual harm [GartonAsh2016]

Two Feminist Frameworks, Incompatible Conclusions

  • MacKinnon and Dworkin: pornography is eroticized subordination that harms women as a class
  • Anti-censorship feminists: restrictions are paternalistic and instrumentalized for conservative ends
  • Both frameworks have empirical support
  • They produce incompatible policy positions
    • 1986 Meese Commission on pornography produced a report endorsed by anti-pornography feminists and conservative religious groups
    • Alliance that anti-censorship feminists argued revealed whose interests the restrictions actually served [Gray2009, Orenstein2020, Paasonen2011, Tarrant2016]

Regulatory Proposals Affect All Uses of Infrastructure

  • Proposals to restrict one use of digital infrastructure affect all uses of that infrastructure
  • End-to-end encryption protects both criminals and domestic violence survivors, journalists, and dissidents
  • Collateral effects are not incidental
  • They are structural
    • UK Online Safety Act's requirement that platforms scan encrypted messages for illegal content makes end-to-end encryption technically illegal
    • Affecting every private communication on the platform [GartonAsh2016]
