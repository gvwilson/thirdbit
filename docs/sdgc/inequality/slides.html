Who Gets What and Why

"Meritocracy" as a Warning, Not an Aspiration

  • Michael Young coined the term in 1958 as a satire about technocratic hierarchy
  • Intended as a warning
  • Adopted enthusiastically by exactly the people it was meant to warn
  • Word stripped of its original meaning
    • Young wrote an essay in 2001 expressing dismay at how "meritocracy" had been embraced by the very establishment he was warning against [Young1958]

Social Legibility Over Formal Credentials

  • What actually determines hiring is cultural legibility: the references, manners, and assumptions that signal membership
  • Credential matters less than what it says about who you are
  • Pierre Bourdieu called this cultural capital
    • Lauren Rivera's research on elite professional services firms: interviewers primarily selecting for cultural fit, using leisure activities as proxies for class background [Rivera2015]

Class Bias in Hiring Decisions

  • Interviewers consistently rate candidates from higher-income backgrounds as more competent
  • Holds even when CVs are matched for qualifications
  • Bias is not conscious
  • Interviewers believe they are assessing ability
    • UK study sent identical CVs with names suggesting different class backgrounds
    • Working-class-named applicants received fewer callbacks even with identical credentials [SteinLubrano2024]

Why Merit Pay Widens Demographic Gaps

  • Organizations that explicitly adopt merit pay show larger demographic gaps, not smaller
  • Belief in a fair system reduces monitoring for bias
  • The ironic effect of meritocracy
    • Emilio Castilla found that organizations with explicit merit pay showed larger racial and gender wage gaps than those without
    • Because people stopped checking for bias [Sandel2020, Wilkinson2011]

The Invisible Prior Filter

  • Access to the credentials that signal merit is itself unequally distributed
  • Exam scores test preparation as much as ability
  • Preparation costs money
  • Those seeing only the test results do not see the prior filter
    • SAT scores correlate with family income far more strongly than they correlate with college performance [Karabel2006]

What "Just" Conceals

  • "Why don't you just open a bank account?" conceals requirements for ID, a fixed address, and a deposit
  • "Just" implies a simple action
  • Action only simple from inside the system
  • Cascading prerequisites invisible to those who have never needed to navigate them
    • In some US cities, getting a government ID to open a bank account requires a bank account to pay the fee
    • Circular requirement that takes weeks of bureaucratic navigation to break [Eubanks2018]

The Invisible Knapsack

  • Peggy McIntosh: privilege operates as an invisible set of advantages
  • Advantages unnoticeable to those who have them precisely because their absence has never caused a problem
  • Listing the contents of the knapsack makes the invisible visible
    • McIntosh's 1989 essay listed 50 specific privileges she could exercise as a white person
    • Used in diversity training worldwide for three decades [McIntosh1989]

Scarcity Depletes Cognitive Bandwidth

  • Mullainathan and Shafir: poverty imposes cognitive load that reduces bandwidth available for other decisions
  • People in scarcity not making worse decisions because of character
  • They are cognitively depleted
  • "Just do X" response ignores the bandwidth cost of managing scarcity
    • Experiments found that inducing financial worry temporarily reduced cognitive performance by 13 IQ points
    • Equivalent to losing a night of sleep [MullainathanShafir2013]

When Workarounds Hide Structural Failures

  • When a capable individual navigates a broken system, the system appears to work
  • Individual workarounds make structural failures invisible to those with the power to fix them
  • Person who can "just do X" never learns the system is broken for those who can't
    • Eubanks documented how social workers who mastered Indiana's welfare automation system became cheerleaders for a system that denied benefits to thousands of legitimate claimants [Eubanks2018]

Who We Think Belongs in Which Roles

  • George Gerbner: heavy media consumption shapes viewers' sense of who occupies which roles
  • Children who watch more television have more stereotyped views of occupational roles
  • Representation of roles in media shapes who believes those roles are available to them
    • Exposure to fictional women engineers increased women's interest in engineering careers [Tripp2015]

The Representation Feedback Loop

  • Visible role models shape who believes a path is available, which shapes who pursues it, which shapes future representation
  • Breaking the loop requires deliberate intervention, not just removing formal barriers
  • Loop is self-perpetuating
  • Does not correct automatically
    • Proportion of women graduating with CS degrees in the US peaked at 37% in 1984 and has declined since, despite women outperforming men in most other STEM fields [Gray2009, OmiWinant2015, Tripp2015]

How Algorithms Raise Barriers for Underrepresented Creators

  • Recommendation algorithms surface content similar to prior engagement
  • Prior engagement reflects the representation that existed before the platform
  • Underrepresented creators face an algorithmic headwind built from historical exclusion
    • Black musicians on Spotify receive fewer algorithmic playlist placements than their streaming numbers would predict
    • Pattern documented in a 2021 analysis [Cottom2017]

Structural Design Can Produce Representation

  • Rwanda's post-genocide constitution required 30% women in parliament
  • Country now leads the world in female legislative representation, with over 60% of seats
  • Result came from a constitutional mandate, not from cultural change or gradual progress
  • Structural rules produce structural outcomes [Tripp2015]
