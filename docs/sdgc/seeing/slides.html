Reading the World

The World You Are Inheriting

  • The adults in charge are running out of time
  • You will be responsible sooner than you think
  • This course is about understanding how power works

Progress Was Never Automatic

  • 200 years ago almost every society practiced slavery
    • Brazil abolished slavery in 1888 and Saudi Arabia not until 1962
  • 100 years ago women were not legally persons in many countries
    • In France, married women could not open a bank account without their husband's written permission until 1965
  • Every gain required organized political effort

You Are Already Political

  • Refusing to engage does not remove you from politics
  • Every technical decision encodes values
  • The question is whether those decisions are made consciously

What This Course Does and Why

  • This course spends most of its time diagnosing how power operates and how harm accumulates
  • That order is deliberate: understanding the mechanism is necessary before evaluating the intervention
  • Final decks ("Regulation That Worked", "What Changes and How") address what has actually succeeded
  • Evidence for those successes is more convincing once you understand what they were working against

Harm That Accumulates Slowly

  • Most serious harm is diffuse, cumulative, and slow to emerge
    • Leaded gasoline lowered the IQ of an entire generation of children, but no single tank of fuel caused a measurable injury
  • Individual instances easy to dismiss as acceptable risk
  • Cumulative picture harder to deny
  • And easier to delay
    • Took decades to build public pressure around tobacco, lead paint, and vehicle safety [Oreskes2010, Perrow1999, Singer2023]

The Cognitive Pollution Model

  • Platforms produce radicalization and mental health damage by design, not accident
    • Facebook's own internal research documented harm to teenage girls' body image
    • Company suppressed the findings for years
  • Harm is a byproduct of the incentive structure, not a malfunction
  • Calling it "pollution" clarifies who bears the cost and who should be responsible

Manufacturing Doubt: An Industry Strategy

  • Tobacco, lead, and ozone research all contested by funded counter-research
    • France, Belgium, and Italy's asbestos industries ran coordinated doubt campaigns through the 1980s and 1990s, delaying national bans by a decade [Oreskes2010]
  • Goal was delay, not refutation
  • Manufacturing uncertainty is cheaper than changing behavior

Who Funds the Research

  • Industry-funded research consistently finds industry-favorable results
    • In the 1960s, the Sugar Research Foundation funded studies attributing obesity to fat rather than sugar, shaping public health guidance for decades [Michaels2008]
  • Funding shapes what questions are asked and what counts as sufficient evidence
  • Transparency about funding is necessary but not sufficient
    • Even disclosed funding biases results because it shapes which hypotheses get tested

Beliefs as Identity Signals

  • People hold contested beliefs as markers of group membership, not conclusions from evidence
  • Correcting a factual error can feel like an attack on identity
    • A smoker in 1965 accepting the cancer evidence meant implicitly judging every friend they had ever offered a cigarette to [Hoffer2010, SteinLubrano2024]
  • Not a failure of intelligence
  • It is what social brains do

Motivated Reasoning

  • People scrutinize arguments that threaten their preferred conclusions more skeptically
    • A gun-owning scientist will examine gun violence research more skeptically than climate research if their social circle treats gun ownership as identity-defining [Achen2017, Robin2018]
  • Data is cheap cover for decisions already made on other grounds
    • Think tanks produce reports full of statistics not to change minds but to create the appearance of rigor
  • Motivated reasoning is not dishonesty
  • People genuinely believe their scrutiny is rigorous

Stories Move People; Statistics Don't

  • A single identified life changes behavior in ways that statistics do not
    • Donation studies find that people give more to help one named child than to address aggregate mortality statistics covering millions [Kahneman2011]
  • Stories transmit emotional and social context that numbers cannot
  • Effective advocates choose narrative over data when both are available

What Actually Changes Minds

  • Alter the social context, not the information
    • Smoking rates fell fastest when it became socially unacceptable at work and in restaurants, not when warnings appeared on packages
  • Find trusted messengers inside the target community
  • Reframe the issue rather than adding more evidence

What This Course Mostly Does Not Do

  • This course delivers information to people who are already somewhat sympathetic
  • Real limitation: information alone rarely changes minds, and we just told you that
  • Goal is not to change your mind directly
  • Goal is to help you understand well enough to find trusted messengers, alter local contexts, and reframe the argument effectively in your own networks
