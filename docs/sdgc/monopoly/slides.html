Money, Monopoly, and Bubbles

Where Money Actually Comes From

  • Most people believe central banks print money and lend it to banks
  • This is backwards: commercial banks create money when they make loans
    • London goldsmiths discovered this in the 1660s: they could issue more paper receipts than they had gold in their vaults, and merchants preferred the paper [McLeay2014]
  • Money is destroyed when loans are repaid

Banks Create Money When They Lend

  • A bank loan creates a deposit simultaneously with the loan
    • Bank of England confirmed this explicitly in a 2014 paper: loan and deposit appear on the bank's books at the same moment [Chang2012, Graeber2011, McLeay2014]
  • Deposit is new money
  • Did not exist before the loan
  • Credit relationships predate money, which emerged to settle debts rather than to replace barter

Why Financial Crises Make Sense

  • Banks recognizing losses reduces the money supply
  • Credit contraction causes economic contraction
    • September 2007: three-day queues outside Northern Rock branches
    • First run on a British bank in 150 years
    • Triggered by mortgage exposure doubts
  • Mechanism explains why financial crises feel like the economy "shrinking"

New Tech, Old Regulatory Gaps

  • Fintech companies capture the profit of money creation without the regulatory responsibilities banks carry
    • Buy-now-pay-later firms charge effective interest rates far exceeding what licensed banks can legally charge, while escaping consumer credit regulations
  • "Move fast and break things" often means operate before regulation catches up
  • Regulatory gap is a competitive advantage, not an accident

The Standard Oil Playbook

  • Rockefeller used railroad rebates to undercut competitors until they sold or failed
    • Standard Oil controlled ~90% of US oil refining capacity at its peak
    • Achieved through secret rebates and predatory pricing, not superior efficiency [Stoller2019]
  • Goal was control of the market, not selling a better product
  • Winning by eliminating competitors is a different strategy from winning by competing
    • The 1911 Supreme Court breakup actually increased Rockefeller's personal fortune as successor companies' stock prices rose

Network Effects and Self-Reinforcing Monopolies

  • A network is more valuable to each user as more users join
  • First-mover advantage compounds: the largest network is the most valuable
    • Facebook acquired Instagram for $1B in 2012 and WhatsApp for $19B in 2014
    • Eliminating rivals before they matured into competitive threats
  • Switching costs rise as users invest more in a platform

What Antitrust Law Got Wrong

  • Consumer welfare standard evaluates mergers by short-term price effects
    • Under this standard, a platform can acquire potential competitors at any price as long as consumer prices don't immediately rise [Khan2017, Shapiro1999, Wu2010]
  • Platforms can acquire competitors before they pose a competitive threat
  • Mechanism of harm is market structure, not immediate consumer price

The Three Stages of Enshittification

  • Stage one: subsidize users to build the base
    • Booking.com launched in 2005 offering 12% commission and genuinely favorable terms for hotels [Doctorow2022, Doctorow2025, Sapp2026]
  • Stage two: subsidize business customers using the captive user base as leverage
  • Stage three: harvest both once each group is locked in
    • By 2019, Booking.com was charging hotels 25-30% per booking plus fees for "preferred placement"

Subsidy, Then Lock-In, Then Extraction

  • Subsidy phase is infrastructure investment, not generosity
    • Amazon sold the Kindle Fire tablet at a loss in 2011
    • Loss was the price of capturing a captive market for digital purchases
  • Investor pressure demands extraction once dominance is achieved
  • Columbia House ran this play in the 1950s and Booking.com ran it in the 2010s

Public Infrastructure as Structural Remedy

  • India's UPI and Brazil's Pix show that payment infrastructure can be public
    • India's UPI processed over 8 billion transactions per month by 2023, more than Visa and Mastercard combined in the country
  • Interoperability mandates require platforms to accept competing connections
  • These are structural remedies
  • Appeals to corporate behavior are not

How Bubbles Grow

  • Bubbles grow from real phenomena but disconnect price from underlying value
  • Participants don't need to believe prices are justified
  • Only that someone else will pay more
    • This logic is called the "greater fool theory": you overpay because you expect to find a more willing buyer [Galbraith1954, Kindleberger2005, Mackay1841, Shiller2015]
  • Tulip mania, the dot-com bubble, and crypto followed the same dynamics

Who Pays When Bubbles Burst

  • Crashes destroy capital but often leave infrastructure that later proves useful
    • Dot-com crash eliminated most companies but left behind fiber-optic cable that the next generation of internet services built on
  • Losses fall on workers and ordinary households rather than on the institutions that created the instruments
    • 2008 crash eliminated trillions in household wealth while banks that created mortgage-backed securities were bailed out [JohnsonKwak2010, Lewis2010]
  • Asymmetry between who profits and who loses is not accidental
