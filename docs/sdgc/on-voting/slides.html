Elections, Courts, and Democratic Erosion

Voting Systems Are Not Neutral

  • The method used to count votes determines who wins as much as who votes
  • Plurality voting (first past the post) produces different outcomes than ranked-choice voting or proportional systems
  • A system that seems fair because every ballot counts once can still systematically advantage some voters over others
    • New Zealand switched from first-past-the-post to proportional representation in 1996
    • Voter turnout rose and parliament became more representative

The Problem with Plurality Voting

  • In a three-way race, a candidate can win with 34% of the vote while 66% preferred someone else
  • Produces spoiler effects, strategic voting, and underrepresentation of smaller parties
  • Countries using proportional representation have higher voter turnout, more representative parliaments, and lower polarization
    • UK Green Party won 7% of the vote in 2024 and 4 seats
    • A proportional system would have given them roughly 46 seats [Achen2017]

Gerrymandering Is Structural Corruption

  • The party controlling a state legislature draws district boundaries for the next decade
  • Sophisticated mapping software can guarantee a party 60% of seats with 50% of the vote
  • Not occasional abuse
  • Predictable consequence of who controls the line-drawing process
    • After the 2010 census, Republican state legislators used REDMAP software to draw districts so precisely that Democrats won 51% of Wisconsin's state legislative votes but only 39% of seats

How Gerrymandering Works

  • "Packing" concentrates opposition voters into a few districts they win by huge margins
  • "Cracking" divides remaining opposition voters across many districts where they lose narrowly
  • Result is a stable legislative majority that no single election can reverse
    • North Carolina's congressional map redrawn six times between 2010 and 2022 by courts finding unconstitutional partisan manipulation
    • Each time the party in power drew a new map defending the same structural advantage

Money in Politics Is Structural

  • Citizens United v. FEC (2010) held that political spending is protected speech
  • Corporations and wealthy donors can spend unlimited sums through independent expenditure committees
  • Result is a system in which a handful of donors shape what legislation is proposed more than millions of ordinary voters [Achen2017]

Dark Money

  • 501(c)(4) organizations can spend on political advertising without disclosing their donors
  • Donor is invisible
  • The advertisement is visible
  • The entity that actually funds a candidate can maintain public distance from their positions [Mayer2016]
    • Jane Mayer's Dark Money documented how the Koch brothers' network of nonprofits spent over $400M influencing the 2012 elections without any public disclosure of the source

Voter Suppression as Legal Strategy

  • Reducing voting hours, closing polling places, requiring specific ID documents, and purging voter rolls are all legal in many jurisdictions
  • These measures reduce turnout selectively: they fall hardest on populations with less flexible schedules, less access to transportation, and less geographic proximity to polling places
  • They function as structural barriers independent of any individual's intent [Anderson2018]
    • After the Supreme Court gutted the Voting Rights Act in 2013, states with a history of discriminatory practices implemented new restrictions within hours of the ruling

Democratic Backsliding Follows a Pattern

  • Hungary (2010–), Turkey (2013–), and India (2014–) followed similar paths: electoral victory, then legal changes to entrench the winning party, then pressure on courts, media, and civil society
  • Democratic erosion usually happens through legal means
  • Does not look like a coup [LevitskyZiblatt2018]
    • Viktor Orbán used a parliamentary supermajority to rewrite Hungary's constitution, redraw electoral districts, and install loyalists on the constitutional court
    • All within existing legal processes

How Democracies Die Without a Coup

  • Legislature passes laws that constrain opponents
  • Courts filled with loyalists who uphold those laws
  • Media pressured, purchased, or harassed into compliance
  • Civil society organizations defunded or harassed into irrelevance
  • Elections continue, but the contest is no longer fair
    • Steven Levitsky and Daniel Ziblatt identified this pattern across Hungary, Venezuela, Turkey, and Peru in their 2018 book How Democracies Die [LevitskyZiblatt2018]

Selectorate Theory and Democratic Capture

  • As described earlier, leaders maintain power by satisfying a winning coalition large enough to govern but small enough to reward privately
  • Democracies with large winning coalitions must provide broad public goods
  • When gerrymandering and voter suppression shrink the effective winning coalition, the logic shifts toward private goods for core supporters
    • Bueno de Mesquita's selectorate theory predicts that as the winning coalition shrinks, public goods investment falls and private transfers to supporters rise [BuenodeMesquita2011]

Courts Are Political Institutions

  • US Supreme Court decides cases through a nomination and confirmation process that is explicitly political
  • Federalist Society coordinated three decades of law school recruitment, clerkship placement, and judicial nomination to reshape the federal judiciary
  • Same infrastructure and patience is available to any organized coalition
    • Federalist Society founded in 1982 with seed funding from conservative donors
    • By 2020, all six conservative Supreme Court justices were members or alumni

Roe Was Reversed by a Thirty-Year Project

  • Reversal of Roe v. Wade in 2022 was not a sudden event
  • Result of sustained judicial nominations, strategic case selection, and law school pipeline development that began in the 1970s
  • Treating it as a surprise is a failure of historical analysis
    • Legal strategy that reversed Roe was written out in detail in Federalist Society publications years before the cases that accomplished it were filed [Mayer2016]

Dual-Class Shares as Private Governance Capture

  • As described earlier, tech companies use dual-class share structures to entrench founder control regardless of shareholder preferences
  • A founder with 10% of equity can control 80% of votes
  • Regulatory question is whether this private concentration has public consequences: it clearly does when the platform controls information flows for billions of people
    • Mark Zuckerberg controls approximately 57% of Meta's voting power while owning roughly 13% of its equity

What Has Actually Worked

  • Ranked-choice voting used in Ireland, Australia, New Zealand, and over a dozen US cities; has not produced chaos
  • Independent redistricting commissions have reduced gerrymandering in Arizona, California, and Michigan
  • Public matching funds for small donations have shifted donor incentives in New York City
  • Constitutional revision through broad public participation has succeeded in Iceland and Scotland

The 3.5% Threshold Again

  • As described earlier, sustained participation by roughly 3.5% of a population has been sufficient to force political change
  • Electoral organizing, legal challenges, and constitutional campaigns have all worked when that threshold of organized, sustained pressure was reached
  • Structure of the problem is the same as every other power contest in this course
  • The specific tools are different
