What Drives Behavior

The Rational Actor Model Is Wrong

  • Standard economics assumes people calculate optimal choices
  • Decades of experimental evidence show they don't
  • Richard Thaler won the Nobel Prize for behavioral economics in 2017: mainstream for a generation
  • The lag between what economists now know and what Silicon Valley tech bros tell each other is one of the reasons these slides exist
  • Policy built on wrong models fails in predictable ways

Framing Changes Choices

  • The same option described differently produces different decisions
    • Kahneman and Tversky: rephrasing "200 lives saved" as "400 deaths" reversed majority preference [Kahneman2011]
  • 90% survival rate and 10% death rate are logically identical
  • People choose differently depending on which they hear
  • Platforms exploit this systematically through interface design
    • Default opt-in vs. opt-out shifts organ donation rates from under 15% to over 90% across European countries [Thaler2009]

Loss Aversion and Cognitive Shortcuts

  • Losses feel roughly twice as bad as equivalent gains feel good
    • NYC taxi drivers work longer hours on slow days and quit early on good ones: managing losses, not maximizing income [Kahneman2011]
  • People anchor to arbitrary reference points and adjust insufficiently
    • Participants who spun a wheel landing on 65 guessed 45% more African countries in the UN than those whose wheel landed on 10, despite knowing the wheel was random
  • Availability bias makes vivid recent events feel more probable than they are

How Platforms Exploit Choice Architecture

  • Default options chosen far more often than alternatives
    • Austria's opt-out organ donation system: above 99% consent
    • Germany's opt-in: under 15% [Thaler2009]
  • Nudges change behavior without changing available options
  • Engagement optimization is applied choice architecture at scale
    • Every interface decision nudges toward more time spent
    • What to show first, notification framing, scroll boundaries: all optimized for engagement

Are Humans Naturally Selfish?

  • Self-interest model predicts people accept any positive offer in the ultimatum game
  • They don't: most reject offers they consider unfair, at cost to themselves
    • In some Indigenous Amazonian communities, participants rejected both too-low and too-high offers as violations of expected generosity norms
  • People are willing to pay to punish unfairness

The Ultimatum Game

  • One player proposes a split
  • The other accepts or rejects
    • If the second player rejects, neither player gets anything: rejection is a costly punishment for perceived unfairness
  • Offers below about 30% are routinely rejected across cultures
  • Run with real money across dozens of countries
  • Basic finding holds

What Social Darwinism Got Wrong

  • "Survival of the fittest" applied to human competition by people who misread Darwin
    • The phrase is Herbert Spencer's from 1864, not Darwin's
    • Darwin described differential reproductive success, not competitive dominance
  • Natural selection favors cooperation within groups as well as competition between them
  • Social Darwinism was political argument dressed as natural science
    • Carnegie and Rockefeller both cited it to justify opposition to unions and social programs

When Commons Work

  • "Tragedy of the commons" describes a specific failure mode, not an inevitability
  • Elinor Ostrom documented sustainable commons governance across fisheries, forests, and irrigation systems
    • Won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2009: first woman to receive it [Bowles2011, Hardin1968, Ostrom2015]
  • Well-governed commons outperform both private ownership and state management in many contexts

Status Matters More Than Absolute Wealth

  • Above a threshold, additional income does not increase well-being
  • Relative position within a comparison group determines satisfaction
    • Norway made tax records publicly searchable in 2001
    • Well-being gap between higher- and lower-income Norwegians widened by 29% without any change in absolute incomes [Boyce2010, Frank1985, PerezTruglia2020]
  • Social standing is what people actually compete for

Conspicuous Consumption

  • Spending to signal rank is a different activity from spending for utility
    • A luxury watch tells time no better than a ten-dollar one
    • Entire price premium is the signal
  • Value of positional goods lies in their costliness to others, not their function
  • Veblen described this in 1899
  • Mechanism unchanged [Veblen1899]
    • Conspicuous overwork now the primary professional status signal
    • Midnight email and weekend calls replace conspicuous leisure

Positional Goods Cannot Be Democratized

  • If everyone has a positional good, it ceases to signal rank
  • Making positional goods widely available destroys the value being competed for
    • When business casual spread to offices in the 1990s, bespoke-suit wearers simply moved on to something else
  • Competition for status is structurally zero-sum [Hirsch2015]

The Global Comparison Problem

  • Local comparison groups have natural upper bounds
    • In a village or workplace, you can only encounter so many people who earn more than you
  • Platforms replaced bounded local comparison with an unlimited global feed
    • Instagram surfaces the wealthiest, most photogenic, most successful outliers from among billions of users
  • The keeping-up problem becomes structurally unsolvable
    • No income growth can close a gap defined by the world's extremes [Wilkinson2011]
