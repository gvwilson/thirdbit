Governance, Assets, and Permissions

  • Deliberate closure first: less traumatic, and the practices are good project hygiene anyway
  • Governance
    • Every team needs explicit decision-making rules (e.g., BDFL or Martha's Rules)
    • Rules must include suffrage: who can veto, not just who is consulted
    • Few projects have a GOVERNANCE.md file; fewer still include shutdown criteria
    • Add shutdown criteria before they are needed; discussing them may surface pre-existing disagreements, not cause new ones
    • The Apache Software Foundation's attic process is a documented model: at least three active respondents required to keep a project viable
    • Write conditions for shutdown, who has authority to make the call, what assets must be preserved, and what obligations must be honoured while the project is still healthy
      • Once a project is under stress, governance discussions become much harder details
    • A closure plan that sits in a drawer and has never been tested is a fantasy document
      • Treat it as a drill script and walk through it periodically to check that each step is actually executable with the people available details
  • Assets and obligations
    • Code, data, discussion archives, website, social media accounts, domain name, hardware, trademarks, name recognition, community goodwill
    • Hosting costs, software licenses, bank accounts, partially-completed contracts, funding reports, confidential data obligations
    • Legal responsibilities may attach to both; ask someone before assuming
    • Modern project infrastructure is often tightly coupled in ways that only become visible during shutdown (e.g., one account that pays for ten services)
      • Treat any single point of control that cannot survive 48-hour unavailability as a bug details
    • Handing over a GitHub organization and a domain name is not the same as handing over governance obligations or data privacy commitments
    • Every handover should include an explicit list of what is and is not being transferred details
  • Permissions
    • Use dedicated organizational accounts rather than personal ones as soon as multiple people have privileged access
    • Keep a findable list of privileged actions and the accounts that can perform them
    • Use a password manager for all organizational credentials

Exercise: Project Inventory

Work individually for ten minutes, then share in groups of three or four:

  1. Make a list of everything that belongs to your project: code, data, accounts, hardware, mailing lists, social media handles. Compare lists with your group: what did others include that you missed?

  2. What would your project be unable to do if you were unavailable for three months?

  3. Which of your project's activities depend on personal accounts rather than accounts dedicated to the project?
