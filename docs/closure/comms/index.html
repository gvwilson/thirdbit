Communicating a Deliberate Shutdown

  • Tell people what you know, as soon as you know it
    • Information gaps cause more anxiety than difficult truths
    • Conflicting verbal and written instructions cause lasting harm
    • Indirect costs (lost contributors, cancelled collaborations, management distraction) are larger than direct ones
    • The information vacuum does more damage than the honest announcement would have done details
  • Framing matters: "putting into hibernation" provokes less hostility than "termination" and keeps the door open for revival
    • Leaders leave the organizations they built as Monarchs (refuse to leave, undermine successors), Generals (depart reluctantly, attempt to return), Ambassadors (leave willingly, remain available), or Governors (leave cleanly)
    • Recognizing which pattern applies shapes the handover conversations details
  • Don't start by announcing shutdown
    • Announce that you are stopping work and invite known successors to take over
    • Do not accept successors you don't already know: supply chain attack risk
  • Give users real deadlines and enough time to act: two to six months between announcement and shutdown worked well for most platforms
  • Use the project's own announcement channels, not just the repository README; a README update reaches developers, not end users
  • What to tell people: reasons (as much as you can), timelines, what happens to assets
    • Structured conversations with funders, institutional sponsors, and team members to establish what matters most to each stakeholder, what the least bad outcome looks like for each party, and what trade-offs each is willing to make
    • Focus on what people actually want, not only on what is technically possible details
  • Create a CLOSURE.md or HANDOVER.md covering:
    • Key achievements
    • Current state (working, broken, incomplete)
    • Where code and data are archived
    • Next steps or alternatives for users
  • What you can legitimately skip: user-facing tutorials, exhaustive dependency documentation, additional tests
  • Hold a team retrospective ("wake"): what worked, what didn't, what will you remember
    • A structured exit interview oriented toward what should survive rather than what went wrong
    • More than a conventional lessons-learned report because it is personal, narrative, and explicitly aimed at what continues details

Exercise: Write a Shutdown Notice

  1. In pairs, draft a short announcement (five to eight bullet points) for your own project or for Vaida's erosion data project. The announcement should name the reasons for closure, give a concrete timeline, explain what will happen to the data and the website, and point users to alternatives or successors.

  2. After drafting, swap with another pair and identify one thing the other pair's notice does well and one thing it could improve.
