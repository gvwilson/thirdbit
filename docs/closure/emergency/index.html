Emergency Closure

  • Abrupt closure means you get one shot and cannot correct course
    • Under time pressure, experts do not compare options; they recognize a familiar type and act
    • Asking "has anyone seen something like this before?" activates usable tacit knowledge details
  • Cosmology episodes occur when governance structures that normally coordinate work suddenly disappear
    • Ensure fallback communication channels (personal email addresses, shared documents) exist before they are needed details
  • First step: establish exactly how much time you have and what constraints are non-negotiable
    • Organizations sometimes keep shutdown news secret to prevent morale collapse
    • This backfires: rumors and information vacuums are more destructive than bad news
    • Silence, denial, and diminishment all increase reputational and community harm
    • A single paragraph saying "here is what we know, here is what we do not know, here is when we expect more information" is not a plan but prevents the harms of silence details
  • Triage ruthlessly using the MoSCoW method: must do, should do, could do, won't do
    • Must do: obligations that carry legal or financial risk if unmet
    • Should do: the minimum viable record of the project's work
    • Could do: give the community a way to find each other after you are gone
    • Won't do: everything else, including anything that amounts to "do it properly"
    • Under acute stress, cognitive load narrows attention to the most visible problem
    • Spend the first few minutes of a crisis writing down every category of thing that must be addressed before attempting to resolve any of them details
  • "A README and a container run command" is a viable minimum handover for research software with working infrastructure
  • Notify funders and stakeholders with binding claims before the project goes dark
  • Provide a forwarding address: even one paragraph in the repository README is better than silence
  • Resist the pressure to promise maintenance you cannot deliver
    • The first conversations after an unplanned closure announcement should promote safety, calm, self-efficacy, connectedness, and hope before turning to logistics details
    • Team members respond to the same event in problem-focused and emotion-focused modes simultaneously
    • Tasks requiring immediate action should be handled by whoever is in a problem-focused state, and social coordination must not wait until logistics are resolved details
  • People on visas face disproportionate risk: loss of employment can trigger immigration consequences with no remedy
  • Engineers sometimes self-organize to do preservation work without management mandate; if you can support this without endangering yourself, do so
    • Community members converge on a crisis without being told to
    • Recognize this as a resource and find ways to coordinate with it rather than ignoring it details
  • Members of marginalized groups often have more practice navigating hostile bureaucracies; their tactics can be instructive
  • When formal procedures are inapplicable because time or infrastructure is unavailable, the people present should act within their own discretion rather than waiting for authorization they cannot obtain details

Exercise: Triage Under Pressure

Use Liam's scenario (end-of-week shutdown of an open source tool):

  1. List everything that would need to be done to close the project properly.

  2. Apply MoSCoW: sort each item into must, should, could, or won't, given a five-day deadline and one person working half-time.

  3. Identify the single most important thing Liam can do in the first two hours.
