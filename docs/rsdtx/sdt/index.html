Signal Detection Theory Analysis

The Problem

  • In a detection experiment, an observer responds "yes" or "no" to each stimulus; some stimuli contain a signal, others do not.
  • Raw accuracy conflates two separate factors: the observer's perceptual sensitivity to the signal and their tendency to say "yes" regardless.
  • Signal detection theory (SDT) separates these factors by modeling the internal evidence as two normal distributions and computing:
  • $d'$ (d-prime): the distance between the distributions, measuring sensitivity.
  • Criterion ($c$): the threshold the observer uses to decide, measuring response bias.
  • An ROC curve: the tradeoff between hit rate and false-alarm rate across all possible thresholds.

An observer responds "yes" on 80% of signal trials and 80% of noise trials. What does this tell you about their sensitivity?

Their sensitivity d' is high because they correctly identify most signals.
Wrong: a high hit rate alone does not indicate good sensitivity; this observer also says "yes" on 80% of noise trials, so they are simply responding "yes" to everything and cannot discriminate signal from noise.
Their sensitivity d' is zero because they cannot discriminate signal from noise.
Correct: d' = z(HR) - z(FAR) = z(0.8) - z(0.8) = 0; equal hit and false-alarm rates indicate the observer is performing at chance.
Their sensitivity d' is negative because they sometimes miss signals.
Wrong: d' is negative only when HR < FAR, meaning the observer inverts the response; here HR = FAR, so d' = 0.
Their sensitivity depends on the number of trials.
Wrong: d' is computed from rates, not raw counts, and does not change with the number of trials as long as the rates remain the same.

The Equal-Variance Gaussian Model

  • The noise distribution represents the internal evidence when no signal is present: $\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.
  • The signal distribution represents the evidence when a signal is present: $\mathcal{N}(d', 1)$.
  • Both distributions have unit variance; only their means differ.
  • The observer responds "yes" whenever the evidence exceeds a criterion $c$.
  • Hit rate: $\text{HR} = P(\text{evidence} > c \mid \text{signal}) = 1 - \Phi(c - d')$
  • False alarm rate: $\text{FAR} = P(\text{evidence} > c \mid \text{noise}) = 1 - \Phi(c)$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

Computing d-prime and Criterion

  • Inverting the two rate equations:
  • $\Phi^{-1}(\text{HR}) = d' - c$
  • $\Phi^{-1}(\text{FAR}) = -c$
  • Subtracting: $d' = \Phi^{-1}(\text{HR}) - \Phi^{-1}(\text{FAR})$
  • Averaging: $c = -\tfrac{1}{2}(\Phi^{-1}(\text{HR}) + \Phi^{-1}(\text{FAR}))$
  • $c = 0$ when the criterion lies exactly midway between the two distributions (unbiased observer); $c > 0$ is conservative, $c < 0$ is liberal.

An observer has a hit rate of 0.841 and a false-alarm rate of 0.159. Using z(0.841) = 1.0 and z(0.159) = -1.0, what is d'?

def d_prime(hit_rate, fa_rate):
    """Return sensitivity (d-prime) from hit and false-alarm rates.

    d' = z(HR) - z(FAR)

    where z = norm.ppf, the inverse of the standard normal CDF.
    d' is the separation between the signal and noise distributions in
    standard deviation units: d' = 0 means no discrimination; d' > 0
    means the observer detects signals above chance; d' < 0 means the
    observer systematically inverts the response.
    """
    return float(norm.ppf(hit_rate) - norm.ppf(fa_rate))


def criterion(hit_rate, fa_rate):
    """Return the response bias (criterion c) from hit and false-alarm rates.

    c = -0.5 * (z(HR) + z(FAR))

    c = 0 for an unbiased observer whose criterion lies exactly midway
    between the noise and signal distributions.  c > 0 is conservative
    (reluctant to respond); c < 0 is liberal (responds even to noise).
    """
    return float(-0.5 * (norm.ppf(hit_rate) + norm.ppf(fa_rate)))

The ROC Curve

  • The ROC curve shows all combinations of (FAR, HR) achievable by an observer with a given $d'$ as the criterion $c$ varies.
  • At a very conservative criterion (large positive $c$): both FAR and HR are near zero (the observer rarely responds).
  • At a very liberal criterion (large negative $c$): both are near one (the observer almost always responds).
  • A diagonal ROC curve (FAR = HR for all $c$) indicates $d' = 0$ (chance); the curve bows toward the upper-left corner as $d'$ increases.
  • The area under the ROC curve (AUC) is the probability that a randomly chosen signal trial produces higher internal evidence than a randomly chosen noise trial; AUC = 0.5 at chance and AUC = 1.0 at perfect sensitivity.
def roc_curve(d_prime_val, n_points=N_ROC_POINTS):
    """Return (far, hr) arrays tracing the ROC curve for a given d-prime.

    The criterion c sweeps from CRITERION_MAX (conservative) to CRITERION_MIN
    (liberal).  At each value of c:

        FAR = 1 - Phi(c)
        HR  = 1 - Phi(c - d')

    where Phi is the standard normal CDF.  The curve runs from near (0, 0)
    to near (1, 1) as c decreases.  Equal performance (chance) produces the
    diagonal FAR = HR; any d' > 0 bows the curve toward the upper-left corner.
    """
    c_values = np.linspace(CRITERION_MAX, CRITERION_MIN, n_points)
    far = 1.0 - norm.cdf(c_values)
    hr = 1.0 - norm.cdf(c_values - d_prime_val)
    return far, hr

Visualizing the ROC Curve

def plot_roc(roc_far, roc_hr, obs_far, obs_hr, d_prime_val, filename):
    """Save an ROC curve with the observed operating point marked."""
    curve_data = [{"far": float(f), "hr": float(h)} for f, h in zip(roc_far, roc_hr)]
    diag_data = [{"far": 0.0, "hr": 0.0}, {"far": 1.0, "hr": 1.0}]
    point_data = [{"far": float(obs_far), "hr": float(obs_hr)}]

    base = alt.Chart().encode(
        x=alt.X("far:Q", title="False alarm rate", scale=alt.Scale(domain=[0, 1])),
        y=alt.Y("hr:Q", title="Hit rate", scale=alt.Scale(domain=[0, 1])),
    )
    curve = base.mark_line(color="steelblue", strokeWidth=2).properties(
        data=alt.Data(values=curve_data)
    )
    diagonal = base.mark_line(strokeDash=[4, 4], color="gray").properties(
        data=alt.Data(values=diag_data)
    )
    operating_point = base.mark_point(
        color="firebrick", size=120, filled=True
    ).properties(data=alt.Data(values=point_data))

    chart = (curve + diagonal + operating_point).properties(
        title=f"ROC curve  (d' = {d_prime_val:.2f})",
        width=360,
        height=360,
    )
    chart.save(filename)

Testing

d-prime is zero when HR equals FAR

  • Equal rates mean the observer cannot discriminate, so $d' = 0$.

d-prime is positive when HR is greater

  • When $\text{HR} > \text{FAR}$ the observer detects signal above chance, so $d' > 0$.

d-prime is negative when HR is less

  • When $\text{HR} < \text{FAR}$ the observer systematically inverts the response, so $d' < 0$.

Known d-prime value

  • $\Phi^{-1}(0.8413) \approx 1.0$ and $\Phi^{-1}(0.1587) \approx -1.0$; so $d' = 1.0 - (-1.0) = 2.0$. Tolerance 0.02 accounts for rounding.

Criterion is zero for an unbiased observer

  • When HR = 0.8413 and FAR = 0.1587, $c = -0.5(1.0 + (-1.0)) = 0$.

ROC starts near the origin

  • At the most conservative criterion (CRITERION_MAX = 4), both FAR and HR are within 0.01 of zero.

ROC ends near (1, 1)

  • At the most liberal criterion (CRITERION_MIN = -4), both FAR and HR are within 0.01 of one.

ROC is monotonically increasing

  • Both FAR and HR must be non-decreasing as the criterion decreases.
import pytest
from sdt import d_prime, criterion, roc_curve


def test_d_prime_zero_when_hr_equals_far():
    # When HR equals FAR the observer cannot discriminate signal from noise,
    # so d' must be zero.
    assert d_prime(0.7, 0.7) == pytest.approx(0.0, abs=1e-6)


def test_d_prime_positive_when_hr_greater():
    # When HR > FAR the observer detects signal above chance, so d' > 0.
    assert d_prime(0.8, 0.3) > 0.0


def test_d_prime_negative_when_hr_less():
    # When HR < FAR the observer inverts the response, so d' < 0.
    assert d_prime(0.3, 0.8) < 0.0


def test_known_d_prime_value():
    # z(0.8413) ≈ 1.0 and z(0.1587) ≈ -1.0, so d' = 1.0 - (-1.0) = 2.0.
    # Tolerance 0.02 accounts for rounding in the boundary probabilities.
    assert d_prime(0.8413, 0.1587) == pytest.approx(2.0, abs=0.02)


def test_criterion_zero_for_unbiased_observer():
    # When the criterion lies midway between the two distributions,
    # z(HR) = -z(FAR), so c = -0.5 * (z(HR) + z(FAR)) = 0.
    # z(0.8413) ≈ 1.0 and z(0.1587) ≈ -1.0, satisfying that condition.
    assert criterion(0.8413, 0.1587) == pytest.approx(0.0, abs=0.01)


def test_roc_starts_near_origin():
    # At the most conservative criterion (CRITERION_MAX = 4) both FAR and HR
    # are near zero.
    far, hr = roc_curve(1.5)
    assert far[0] == pytest.approx(0.0, abs=0.01)
    assert hr[0] == pytest.approx(0.0, abs=0.01)


def test_roc_ends_near_one():
    # At the most liberal criterion (CRITERION_MIN = -4) both FAR and HR
    # approach 1.
    far, hr = roc_curve(1.5)
    assert far[-1] == pytest.approx(1.0, abs=0.01)
    assert hr[-1] == pytest.approx(1.0, abs=0.01)


def test_roc_monotonically_increasing():
    # Both FAR and HR must be non-decreasing as the criterion decreases.
    far, hr = roc_curve(2.0)
    assert all(far[i] <= far[i + 1] for i in range(len(far) - 1))
    assert all(hr[i] <= hr[i + 1] for i in range(len(hr) - 1))

Signal detection theory key terms

Signal detection theory
A framework for separating a participant's perceptual sensitivity from their response tendency by modeling the internal decision variable as two normal distributions and fitting hit rate and false-alarm rate jointly
d-prime (d')
The standardized distance between the signal and noise distributions: d' = z(HR) - z(FAR); d' = 0 at chance, d' > 0 when the observer can discriminate signal from noise
Criterion (c)
The threshold on the internal evidence axis above which the observer responds "yes"; c = -0.5*(z(HR) + z(FAR)); c = 0 is unbiased, c > 0 is conservative, c < 0 is liberal
Hit rate
The proportion of signal trials on which the observer correctly responds "yes": HR = P(yes | signal); also called the true positive rate or sensitivity
False alarm rate
The proportion of noise trials on which the observer incorrectly responds "yes": FAR = P(yes | noise); also called the false positive rate or 1 - specificity
ROC curve
The Receiver Operating Characteristic curve; a plot of hit rate against false-alarm rate as the criterion varies from very conservative to very liberal; summarized by its area (AUC), which equals the probability that the observer assigns higher evidence to a random signal trial than to a random noise trial

Exercises

Area under the ROC curve

The area under the ROC curve (AUC) can be approximated numerically using the trapezoidal rule on the (FAR, HR) arrays returned by roc_curve. Compute AUC for d-prime values of 0, 1, 2, and 3. What is the AUC when d' = 0? What is the relationship between AUC and d' for the equal-variance Gaussian model?

Unequal-variance model

In practice the signal distribution may have a different variance from the noise distribution. Modify roc_curve to accept a second parameter sigma_s (standard deviation of the signal distribution, default 1.0) and compute FAR and HR accordingly. Plot ROC curves for sigma_s values of 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 at a fixed d' = 1.5. How does the shape of the ROC curve change?

Multiple observers

Simulate five observers with d-prime values of 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, and 2.5 and the same neutral criterion (c = 0). Compute the hit rate and false-alarm rate for each and plot all five operating points on the same ROC curve. Which observer lies closest to the diagonal?

Criterion from accuracy and bias

Given overall accuracy (proportion correct) and criterion c, derive formulas for the implied hit rate and false-alarm rate assuming equal numbers of signal and noise trials and c = 0 (neutral bias). Verify numerically with d' = 2.0.
